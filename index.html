CASE STUDY OVERVIEW
📋 THE SCENARIO CyberHealth Security has been hired to conduct a full penetration test on MedTechPro Hospital (MTPH). The hospital runs Electronic Health Records (EHRs), internal communications networks, and IoT-connected medical devices. CyberHealth must uncover vulnerabilities while maintaining strict ethical and operational standards — because any disruption could endanger patients.
HIGH VALUE TARGET

Why Attack MTPH?

Hospitals store rich EHR data (medical, financial, personal) worth thousands per record on the dark web. 24/7 uptime requirements mean any disruption could cost lives, making ransom payments very likely.

DEFINE THIS

What Is a Pen Test?

A simulated cyberattack authorised in writing by an organisation to proactively find exploitable vulnerabilities. Without written authorisation, it is simply illegal hacking — the intent does NOT matter legally.

ETHICS FOCUS

The Ethical Tension

CyberHealth must test live hospital systems without endangering patients or exposing private data. This creates a fundamental tension: the more thorough the test, the greater the risk to patients and data.

CONTEXT

MTPH Technology

The hospital uses EHR databases, internal LANs, IoT medical devices (infusion pumps, cardiac monitors), staff/patient web portals, and email systems — all of which are potential attack surfaces.

SQL INJECTION

SQLi

Malicious SQL inserted into web form inputs to manipulate database queries. Can expose, modify, or delete the entire MTPH patient database. Mitigated by prepared statements and input validation.

XSS

Cross-Site Scripting

Injecting malicious JavaScript into a web page that runs in another user's browser. At MTPH, a stored XSS attack could steal nurse session tokens, allowing impersonation of medical staff.

MEMORY EXPLOIT

Buffer Overflow

Writing beyond a memory buffer's capacity, overwriting adjacent memory to allow arbitrary code execution. Legacy EHR systems at MTPH are particularly vulnerable to this class of attack.

HUMAN FACTOR

Social Engineering

Exploiting human psychology rather than technical flaws. Phishing (deceptive emails), vishing (phone calls), and pretexting (fabricated scenarios) can bypass even the most sophisticated technical defences.

MALWARE TYPES

Malware

Ransomware encrypts files and demands payment — hospitals are prime targets. Spyware silently exfiltrates data. Trojans disguise as legitimate software. Rootkits hide at OS level. Know all four for the exam.

RECON TOOL

Port Scanning & OSINT

Nmap scans reveal all open ports, running services, and device topology. OSINT (LinkedIn, job ads, WHOIS, Google dorking) reveals staff names, tech stack, and exposed files — all from public sources only.

TEST TYPES

Black / White / Grey Box

Black box: no prior knowledge (simulates real attacker). White box: full system access (most thorough). Grey box: partial info — what CyberHealth uses at MTPH, balancing realism with efficiency.

FORENSICS

System Forensics

Post-incident digital analysis to understand exactly what happened, what data was accessed, and how. Used both after real breaches and after penetration tests to document the evidence trail.

ETHICS & PROFESSIONAL STANDARDS
⚠️ EXAM ALERT — HIGH PROBABILITY The 12-mark discussion question almost always targets ethics. You MUST present both sides: why ethical constraints limit testing thoroughness AND why they are non-negotiable in a healthcare context. A one-sided answer cannot score above 6/12.

Authorisation & RoE

Every action requires a signed Rules of Engagement document. Testing outside agreed scope is illegal regardless of intent. No written authorisation = criminal hacking.

Patient Data Confidentiality

Real EHR data must never be extracted or retained. Anonymised synthetic test data must be used. GDPR-equivalent regulations apply — violations carry heavy legal penalties.

Non-Disruption of Care

Testing must never interrupt life-critical systems. Automated scanning must not target ICU monitors or infusion pumps. Manual testing only for patient-critical IoT devices.

Responsible Disclosure

Vulnerabilities are reported only to MTPH. Public disclosure before fixes are applied would be deeply irresponsible. The report itself must be stored and transmitted securely.

Minimising Footprint

Only access what is needed to prove the vulnerability. Going beyond the minimum to prove a point — even within scope — is unethical and potentially grounds for legal action.

Consequences of Breach

Criminal prosecution, civil liability, regulatory fines, reputational destruction, and — most critically — direct patient harm if systems are disrupted during testing.

💡 12-MARK ESSAY STRUCTURE (4 distinct argument blocks) Block 1: Patient safety makes constraints non-negotiable (IoT device risk). Block 2: Legal compliance protects both parties (RoE, computer misuse law). Block 3: Data protection laws constrain data exfiltration. Block 4 (counterargument): Constraints reduce thoroughness — grey/black box misses vulnerabilities white box would find. Conclusion: Constraints are necessary; false assurance from unconstrained testing is more dangerous than the gap in coverage.

EXAM STRATEGY
🎯

ANCHOR EVERY ANSWER TO MTPH

Never give a generic cybersecurity answer. Examiners award marks for contextualised application. Every response should name the hospital, EHRs, IoT medical devices, patient safety, or the CyberHealth team.

📊

MASTER THE COMMAND TERMS

"Identify" = 1 point, no explanation. "Define" = precise technical definition. "Explain" = mechanism + why/how it works. "Describe" = step-by-step walkthrough. "Discuss/Evaluate" = both sides + conclusion required for 8–12 mark questions.

⚡

STRUCTURE YOUR 12-MARK ESSAY

Write at least 4 distinct developed points covering: technical ethics, patient safety, legal implications, and the trade-off between thoroughness and harm avoidance. End with a clear, balanced conclusion. Without a conclusion you cannot reach the top mark band.

🔧

KNOW BLACK / WHITE / GREY BOX COLD

This distinction appears every year. Black = no info (external hacker simulation). White = full access (most thorough). Grey = partial info (CyberHealth's approach at MTPH). Know advantages and disadvantages of each in a hospital context.

📖

ANNOTATE YOUR CASE STUDY COPY

You receive the case study booklet in the exam. Your pre-exam copy should be covered in annotations: PTES phase labels in margins, key terms underlined, ethical issues highlighted. Strong answers cite specific lines from the case study document.

⏱️

TIME MANAGEMENT

Paper 3 is 1 hour. With ~30 marks total, allocate roughly 2 minutes per mark. Spend proportionally more time on the 12-mark essay question. Never overwrite "identify" questions — one clear point per mark is sufficient and wastes less time.
